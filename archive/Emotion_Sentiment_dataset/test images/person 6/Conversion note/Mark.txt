"Can you believe we're finally doing this, Dave?" Mark said, leaning back in his seat and looking out the train window. The green fields of the English countryside blurred by in a soothing rush. "Seems like we've been talking about this trip for a decade."
Dave, 51, chuckled, taking a sip from his travel mug. "More like since we were 20. Back then, it was a grand plan to backpack across Europe on a shoestring budget. Now we've got… well, slightly better bags and slightly more comfortable shoes."
"And slightly more back pain," Mark added with a wince, shifting his position. "This is a good start, though. Just a weekend away in the Lake District. A proper, no-frills boys' trip."
"Exactly. No kids, no wives, no to-do lists," Dave said, a grin spreading across his face. "Just you, me, and a good few pints at the end of the day. You know, it's funny… I was telling Jane the other day, it feels like we're still those two lads from uni, just with more gray hairs and less ambition to stay up past 10 p.m."
Mark laughed. "Speak for yourself, I'm aiming for midnight tonight. But yeah, I know what you mean. We've gone from talking about who's going to be the next big rock star to who's going to get the best deal on car insurance. It's a bit ridiculous, isn't it?"
"It's life, mate," Dave said, his tone softening. "But it's a good one. It's just... nice to press pause on it all for a bit. Remember that time we tried to hitchhike to Glastonbury and ended up sleeping in a bus stop in the rain?"
Mark's eyes lit up. "Oh God, I'd completely forgotten about that! You used your last fiver to buy us two sausage rolls, and we were so proud of them, even though they were stone cold."
"They were the best sausage rolls I've ever had," Dave said, a fond smile on his face. "Here's to making some new, slightly more comfortable memories."
He raised his travel mug, and Mark clinked his against it. "To new memories," Mark echoed, "and to not having to sleep in any bus stops."

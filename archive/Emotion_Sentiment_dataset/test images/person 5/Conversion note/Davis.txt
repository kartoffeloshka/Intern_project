The history staff room. Mrs. Davis is sitting at her desk, sighing deeply as she shuffles a stack of papers.
(Mrs. Davis, looking exhausted and slightly annoyed - Anger)
Mrs. Davis: Leo? I didn't see you there. Come in. What's all this? You're meant to be at rugby practice.
Leo: Sorry, Mrs. Davis. I just... I had a question about the coursework. I know you've got loads to do.
(Mrs. Davis, her lips curling slightly - Contempt)
Mrs. Davis: A question? At this hour? Honestly, Leo, the deadline is in a week. This is exactly what I've been telling you all term. You can't leave this until the last minute. It shows a complete lack of planning.
Leo: I know, I know. It's just... I'm a bit lost on the primary sources for the First World War topic. The ones you gave us in the textbook, they all seem to say the same thing. I wanted to find something else, something... a bit different.
(Mrs. Davis, scrunching her nose in distaste - Disgust)
Mrs. Davis: "Different"? What do you mean by "different"? Are you implying my carefully curated resources aren't good enough? We've gone over this. Those sources are approved by the exam board. There's no need to go off on some tangent. It's just going to waste your time and likely get you a lower grade.
Leo: No, no, not at all! I just... I found a letter online, a soldier's letter home, and it talked about the food. It's not in the textbook, but it's so vivid. I just thought it was really interesting.
(Mrs. Davis's eyes widen in surprise and alarm - Fear)
Mrs. Davis: You found something online? Without checking with me first? Leo, are you sure it's a reliable source? The internet is a minefield. You have to be so careful. What if it's propaganda? What if it's from some unreliable history blog? You can't just trust anything you read. You could fail your whole coursework for using a non-credible source.
Leo: I checked the site, Mrs. Davis. It's from the Imperial War Museum archives. They've got a whole digital collection. I even cross-referenced it with another site. I think it's authentic.
(Mrs. Davis, her expression softens and a small smile appears - Happy)
Mrs. Davis: The Imperial War Museum? Oh! Well, that's... that's excellent, actually. I didn't know they had that part of their collection digitised. That's a fantastic find, Leo! A soldier's letter about food... that's a brilliant way to add a personal, human element to the analysis. See, this is what I'm talking about. Going beyond the basics. Well done.
(Mrs. Davis, her face returning to a thoughtful, calm state - Neutral)
Mrs. Davis: Alright, so... tell me about this letter. What did it say? How does it fit with your argument? We can't just throw it in for the sake of it. You have to analyse its value and limitations. Let's look at your plan.
Leo: (pulling out his notebook) I was thinking of using it to illustrate the morale of the troops, and how even small things like what they ate had a huge impact. I've written a few bullet points here.
(Mrs. Davis, her brow furrowing with concern - Sad)
Mrs. Davis: Oh, Leo. This... this is all wrong. Your structure is completely off. You've got your introduction, but your body paragraphs jump all over the place. You're not making a clear argument. You're just listing facts. My goodness, have you been paying attention in class?
Leo: I thought... I thought it was okay. I've been struggling to connect everything. It feels like there's so much to say, I don't know where to start or finish.
(Mrs. Davis, her eyes wide with a mix of awe and shock - Surprised)
Mrs. Davis: So you did all this... on your own? And you found that incredible source? Leo, you are so much better than this work suggests. We need to completely restructure this. It's like building a house without a foundation. Give me your notebook. Let's work through it. I'll stay late. It's no trouble.
Leo: You'd do that? Stay late? I thought you were in a rush.
Mrs. Davis: (A brief, reassuring smile) I am, but this is more important. You've got the raw materials for a top-grade piece of work. It would be a crime to let it go to waste. You've shown real initiative here, and that's exactly the kind of independent learning that separates a good student from an excellent one. Right, where's your laptop? Let's start with a new outline. We'll get this sorted out in no time.
